Keyword Research for Beginners: How I Did It for My First SEO Project

Keyword research for beginners sounds simple until you actually sit down to do it. I know because I just went through this exact process for my live SEO project — and the theory only made sense once I applied it to a real page I was trying to rank.

Why I started here, not with writing

It's tempting to jump straight into writing content. I almost did. But without knowing which words people actually search for, you're writing for yourself, not for the people you're trying to reach. Keyword research is what tells you the difference.

The exact steps I followed

1. Seed keywords. I started broad — words directly related to my project, like "SEO," "digital marketing," and "website ranking." These are too competitive to target directly, but they're the starting point everything else branches from.

2. Expanding the list. I typed each seed keyword into Google and noted the autocomplete suggestions, then scrolled to "related searches" at the bottom of the results page. I also used Google Keyword Planner, which is free and shows how many people search a term each month.

3. Checking search volume and difficulty. For each keyword, I looked at two things: how many people search it monthly, and how hard it would be to rank for. As a beginner with a brand-new site, I learned to avoid short, high-competition terms and instead target longer, more specific phrases — long-tail keywords — since they're realistic to actually rank for early on.

4. Checking search intent. Before picking a final keyword, I searched it myself and looked at what was already ranking. If the top results were all beginner guides, that told me people searching this term want to learn a process — so my content needed to match that, not turn it into a sales pitch.

The mistake I almost made

My first instinct was to chase the keyword with the highest search volume I could find. That's a common beginner mistake — high volume also usually means high competition, and a new site has almost no chance of outranking established ones for those terms. Choosing a more specific, lower-competition long-tail keyword was the right call instead.
